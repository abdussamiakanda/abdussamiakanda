Islam and Judaism: A Shared Heritage of Primordial Monotheism

Posted: May 3, 2025
Written by: Md Abdus Sami Akanda

Introduction


According to Jewish literature, all people are born into a core religious tradition that is described as their common heritage. This universal moral religion is regarded as obligatory for all of humanity[1], irrespective of ancestry or particular covenant.

Throughout history, this religion has been identified by various names:
  • Yirat Shamayim (Hebrew: יִרְאַת שָׁמַיִם) - Fearers of Heaven[2]
  • Ger Toshav (Hebrew: גֵּר תּוֹשָׁב) - Resident alien; non-Jews who accept the moral laws of the Torah[3]
  • B'nei Noah (Hebrew: בְּנֵי נֹחַ) - Children of Noah (PBUH); righteous people who observe the Noahide laws[4]
  • Theosebeia (Greek: Θεοσέβεια) - God-fearers; A term used during the Hellenistic period[5]

Association of This Universal Faith with Islam


According to the school of thought of Rabbi Elijah Benamozegh (1823-1900), this common faith corresponds to Islam. Benamozegh, in his work Israel and Humanity, argued that Islam preserves the essential principles of primordial monotheism - the same beliefs Judaism considers binding on all righteous non-Jews[6].

Jewish literature also suggests that during the period of the Second Temple[7], large numbers of non-Jewish believers would come to Jerusalem to offer the Kurban Shlamim (Peace Offering) alongside the Jewish people[8]. The terms Salami, Muslimi, or Muslime appear in some Jewish and Islamic historiographical interpretations as potential references to ancient monotheistic worshipers. These designations remain speculative and are not found in canonical Rabbinic texts but may reflect linguistic or cultural transmission of the root S-L-M (submission/peace).

Primordial Origins of Islam


The Qur'an itself acknowledges the existence of a primordial monotheistic faith, which is considered the original religion of humanity. This is evident in verses that refer to the religion of Abraham (PBUH)[9] and the religion of Noah (PBUH)[10]. The Qur'an also emphasizes the importance of following the teachings of the prophets, including Noah (PBUH), Abraham (PBUH), Moses (PBUH), and Jesus (PBUH), as part of a continuous divine revelation to humanity.

If Islam's foundations are equivalent to what Jewish thought designates as B'nei Noah, then Islam is not merely a religion that began in the 7th century CE. Rather, it represents a continuation of the religion of Noah (PBUH) - and, by extension, the religion of Adam (PBUH)[11].

Historical Recognition of Kinship


The close relationship between Judaism and Islam was acknowledged by Biblical scholars and Jewish authorities for centuries. The Ten Lost Tribes of Israel, various Arab tribes, and groups like the Rechabites were recognized as communities that shared monotheistic beliefs and moral values with ancient Israel[12][13].

This understanding remained largely unchallenged until the emergence of German revisionist scholars in the 19th century, including Julius Wellhausen and Adolph Büchler[14]. These academics introduced theories suggesting that Islam incorporated pagan elements such as moon worship, stone veneration, or asteroid cults[15].

Such claims, however, have been increasingly refuted by both traditional religious scholars and modern historians[16].

Jewish Law's Recognition of Islamic Monotheism


Halakha (Jewish law) holds that Muslims are recognized as perfect monotheists. Islamic theology affirms belief in the same singular, indivisible God recognized in Judaism.[17]

Maimonides (Rambam), in a letter to Obadiah the Proselyte (also in some editions/translations the Mishneh Torah), states explicitly:
"The Ishmaelites are not idol worshippers in the least, and [polytheism] has been long cut off from their mouths and their hearts, and they worship the singular God properly and without any blemish."[18]

Conclusion


The shared heritage of primordial monotheism between Islam and Judaism is a testament to the enduring nature of this faith. The recognition of a common moral code, the acknowledgment of righteous non-Jewish believers, and the historical connections between these two religions highlight the importance of understanding their shared roots.

This understanding fosters respect and dialogue between the two faiths, emphasizing the need for cooperation and mutual understanding in a world divided by religious differences.

  5. Non-Jewish adherents or sympathizers of Judaism who accepted certain moral and religious principles without full conversion. Mentioned in historical and Rabbinic sources as righteous Gentiles. See Wikipedia.
  6. In his major theological work titled "Israel and Humanity", Benamozegh elaborates on the idea that Islam maintains essential truths of ancient monotheism. English translation published by Paulist Press (1995). See Google Books.

Historical and Rabbinic sources note that many righteous Gentiles participated in Temple worship or supported Jewish practices during this era. Pesachim 50a discusses the spiritual value of performing religious duties even without full understanding or pure intent, a principle later applied by Rabbinic commentators to explain why righteous Gentiles were welcomed in certain religious contexts. See also Louis Finkelstein's The Pharisees (Vol. 1, 1962), pp. 132-134.
  8. Kurban Shlamim is a type of sacrificial offering described in Leviticus 3 and Leviticus 7:11-21. While primarily offered by Jews, certain traditions suggest that righteous Gentiles could also participate in peace offerings or bring donations to the Temple.

  11. While the Qur'an does not explicitly mention a "religion of Adam," Islamic and Jewish traditions describe Adam as the first human and the first monotheist, who received direct guidance from God. See Qur'an 2:37 and Genesis 2 and Genesis 3 for the shared Abrahamic narrative.

  13. Rechabites is an ancient Israelite clan known for strict monotheism and ascetic practices, described in Jeremiah 35. They are often cited as an example of righteous non-Israelites or proto-monotheistic groups respected in Biblical tradition.
  14. Julius Wellhausen (1844-1918) was a German biblical scholar known for the Documentary Hypothesis and critical studies of Israelite and Islamic history.
